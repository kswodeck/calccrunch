## Item-by-Item Breakdown

### Bedding ($100–$200)
Dorm beds use **Twin XL** mattresses — longer than a standard twin — so regular sheets won't fit. Budget $40–$80 for two sets of Twin XL sheets (you'll want a spare while one set is in the wash) and $60–$120 for a duvet and cover, which is easier to launder than a comforter and layers better for temperature swings in older dorm buildings.

### Storage & Furniture ($170–$560)
Dorm rooms almost never have enough closet space, which is why under-bed storage bins ($30–$60) show up on every packing list. A rug ($40–$100) adds warmth and cuts down on echo in a room with tile or laminate floors. The biggest optional upgrade here is a desk chair with better lumbar support ($100–$400) — worth it if you'll be studying for hours, skippable if the provided chair is tolerable.

### Room Comfort ($30–$110)
Dorm HVAC is notoriously uneven — some rooms run cold, others run hot. A small desk fan ($20–$40) or space heater ($40–$80) covers either extreme, but **check your dorm's rules first**: many buildings prohibit space heaters and some restrict fans with exposed blades. String lights or a nightlight ($10–$30) round out the category.

### Tech Essentials ($20–$50)
The one item almost every packing list calls a must-buy: a **dorm-safe power strip**. Most dorms ban standard extension cords outright, and rooms often have only one or two outlets for two roommates' worth of electronics.

### Mini Fridge ($60–$150, if allowed)
A compact fridge holds leftovers, drinks, and snacks — genuinely useful if your dining hall hours don't match your schedule. Check your housing agreement for size limits before buying; oversized fridges are a common move-in-day rejection.

### Personal & Health Items ($25–$65)
A reusable water bottle ($15–$50) plus basic grooming supplies (nail clippers, scissors) round out the personal-care line. This is one of the easiest categories to trim by bringing what you already own from home instead of buying new.

## Building Your Move-In Budget

1. **Set a total ceiling first.** Decide whether you're aiming for the $400–$600 core-essentials range or the $700–$900 range with bigger extras, and treat that as a hard cap.
2. **List what you already own.** Towels, a desk lamp, hangers, and basic toiletries usually don't need to be bought new.
3. **Split furniture costs with a roommate.** A mini fridge, rug, or fan is often something two people can share rather than each buying their own.
4. **Buy used where it makes sense.** Furniture and small appliances from a campus marketplace or local classifieds can run 30–50% cheaper than new, and resell easily at the end of the year.
5. **Plug the total into a [budget calculator](/calculators/budget-calculator)** alongside your other back-to-school costs (books, meal plan top-ups, transportation) to see how move-in spending fits your overall semester budget.

## Common Dorm Budget Mistakes

- **Buying everything before seeing the room.** Room dimensions, existing furniture, and outlet placement vary — some purchases (rugs, storage bins sized for a specific bed frame) are better made after move-in.
- **Duplicating what a roommate already has.** Coordinate on shared items like a fridge, fan, or rug before both of you buy one.
- **Ignoring the housing handbook.** Space heaters, certain extension cords, and oversized appliances are commonly banned — check before buying so you're not returning items during move-in week.
- **Skipping the "will I actually use this" test.** Novelty gadgets and extra decor are the easiest place for a $400 budget to creep toward $900+ without adding real value.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How much does it cost to move into a dorm?**
Most families spend $400–$600 on core essentials (bedding, storage, a power strip, basic toiletries) and $700–$900 if adding bigger items like a mini fridge, fan, and headphones.

**What size sheets do I need for a dorm bed?**
Twin XL — 5 inches longer than a standard twin mattress. Regular twin sheets will not fit properly.

**Can I bring a space heater or fan to my dorm?**
Many dorms prohibit space heaters entirely and restrict certain fan types for fire-safety reasons. Always check your housing handbook before buying.

**Is it cheaper to buy dorm furniture used?**
Yes — furniture and small appliances bought secondhand from a campus marketplace or local classifieds typically cost 30–50% less than new, and resell easily at the end of the school year.

**Should I split costs with my roommate?**
For shared-use items like a mini fridge, rug, or fan, splitting the cost (and use) with a roommate is one of the easiest ways to cut your individual move-in budget.
