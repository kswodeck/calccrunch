Figuring out how much gas your road trip will cost takes about 30 seconds once you know the formula. **Fuel cost = (total miles ÷ vehicle MPG) × current price per gallon.** That's it. The rest of this guide walks through a real example, shows you how to build a full road trip fuel budget, and covers the most reliable ways to spend less at the pump.

## The Formula

> **Gas cost = (Miles ÷ MPG) × Price per gallon**

Three inputs. You probably already know two of them (your car's MPG is on the window sticker and in the owner's manual), and gas prices are visible at every station or on an app like GasBuddy.

## A Real Worked Example

**Trip:** Los Angeles to Las Vegas and back — approximately 560 miles round trip.

**Vehicle:** 2022 Honda CR-V, rated 30 MPG highway.

**Gas price assumption:** $4.20 per gallon (a mid-range figure for California — always check current prices before you leave).

**Calculation:**
- Gallons needed: 560 ÷ 30 = **18.67 gallons**
- Fuel cost: 18.67 × $4.20 = **$78.41**

Under $80 in gas for a 560-mile weekend trip. Knowing that number upfront means you can plan the rest of your budget — hotel, food, activities — with confidence.

## Why Real-World MPG Is Lower Than the Window Sticker

The EPA fuel economy rating your car displays is based on standardized test conditions. In the real world, these factors reduce MPG:

- **Air conditioning** — can reduce MPG by 5–25% on hot days
- **City driving and stop-and-go traffic** — highway ratings don't apply
- **Roof racks, cargo carriers, and bike racks** — add aerodynamic drag
- **Speed** — fuel efficiency drops sharply above 60–65 mph; driving at 75 mph uses roughly 20% more fuel than 55 mph
- **Tires** — underinflated tires increase rolling resistance and cut MPG
- **Passengers and luggage weight** — more weight = more fuel

For a long summer road trip with full A/C and loaded luggage, assume 10–15% below your rated highway MPG as a conservative planning figure.

## How to Find the Cheapest Gas Along Your Route

A few reliable strategies:

- **Use GasBuddy or Waze** to find stations with the lowest price along your route — not just the closest. A station two exits ahead can save $0.30/gallon.
- **Fill up before entering urban areas.** Gas is typically cheaper on the outskirts than in city centers.
- **Fill up at membership warehouse clubs** (Costco, Sam's Club) when they're on your route — their fuel prices are consistently lower than the street average.
- **Avoid highway rest-stop stations.** These have some of the highest prices because they face no local competition.
- **Fill the tank the morning before you leave.** You're less likely to pay panic prices when you're not running low on a stretch of highway with no options.

## Electric Vehicles and Hybrids: Same Formula, Different Inputs

If you drive a hybrid, use your real-world combined MPG (often available in your car's fuel economy display after a few tanks). For plug-in hybrids, add the gas-only estimate for the miles you'll drive beyond the electric range.

For pure EVs, the formula shifts to: **(miles ÷ miles per kWh) × electricity cost per kWh.** Most EV drivers use 3–4 miles per kWh in highway conditions. DC fast charging on the road costs more per kWh than home charging, typically $0.35–$0.60/kWh depending on the network and state.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I find my car's MPG?**
It's on the window sticker (if you bought the car new), in the owner's manual, or at fueleconomy.gov. Your car's dashboard trip computer also shows real-time and average MPG — check it after a few highway miles for the most accurate figure.

**Should I use highway or combined MPG for a road trip estimate?**
Use highway MPG for long interstate stretches, combined MPG for trips with mixed driving. If your route goes through cities or has significant stop-and-go, combined is more accurate.

**Does cruise control save gas?**
Yes. Maintaining a steady speed eliminates the small acceleration/deceleration cycles that waste fuel. On a long flat highway, cruise control can improve MPG by 7–14% compared to manually varying your speed.

**Is premium gas worth it?**
Only if your vehicle requires it. If your owner's manual says "premium recommended" (not required), regular 87 is fine. If it says "premium required," using regular can reduce performance and long-term engine health.

**How much does the A/C really cost me?**
On a 500-mile trip at 30 MPG, running A/C full-time (which reduces MPG to roughly 27 MPG) adds about 1.9 gallons — roughly $7–8 at typical gas prices. It's real but modest, and usually worth the comfort on a summer trip.

## The Bottom Line

Calculating road trip fuel cost takes one formula and three numbers you already have. Budget conservatively (use 10–15% below your highway MPG rating), check gas prices in advance with GasBuddy or Waze, and fill up before entering cities or interstates with limited options.
