To convert Celsius to Fahrenheit, multiply by 9/5 (or 1.8) and add 32: **°F = (°C × 9/5) + 32**. For the reverse, subtract 32 then multiply by 5/9: **°C = (°F − 32) × 5/9**. You can plug any number into our free [Celsius to Fahrenheit converter](/calculators/celsius-to-fahrenheit) and get an instant answer — but understanding the formula means you'll never be stuck without a calculator.

## The Exact Formula

The official conversion formula comes from the relationship between the two scales' fixed reference points:

- Water freezes at **0 °C** = **32 °F**
- Water boils at **100 °C** = **212 °F**

The gap of 100 °C spans 180 °F, giving a ratio of 180/100 = **9/5 = 1.8**. That's where the multiplier comes from. The +32 accounts for the offset at the freezing point.

| Direction | Formula | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Celsius → Fahrenheit | °F = (°C × 9/5) + 32 | 20 °C → (20 × 1.8) + 32 = **68 °F** |
| Fahrenheit → Celsius | °C = (°F − 32) × 5/9 | 98.6 °F → (98.6 − 32) × 0.556 = **37 °C** |

## A Fast Mental-Math Shortcut

The exact formula isn't always practical in your head. Here's a two-step approximation that's accurate to within 1–2 degrees:

**Celsius → Fahrenheit (quick):** Double the Celsius temperature, subtract 10%, then add 32.

Example: 25 °C → 25 × 2 = 50 → 50 − 5 = 45 → 45 + 32 = **77 °F** (exact: 77 °F ✓)

**Fahrenheit → Celsius (quick):** Subtract 32, then halve the result and add 10%.

Example: 95 °F → 95 − 32 = 63 → 63 ÷ 2 = 31.5 → 31.5 + 3.15 ≈ **35 °C** (exact: 35 °C ✓)

For casual use — checking the weather, cooking, or travel — this shortcut gets you close enough without a calculator.

## Where Each Scale Is Used

**Celsius** (°C) is the global standard. It's used in:
- Every country except the United States, Liberia, and the Cayman Islands for everyday weather
- Scientific and medical contexts worldwide (body temperature, lab work)
- Cooking in most of the world

**Fahrenheit** (°F) is the dominant scale in:
- The United States for everyday weather and cooking temperatures
- Aviation in some countries (airport weather reports in the US)
- Some older engineering standards

## Why −40 Is the Same in Both Scales

The curiosity that the two scales intersect at −40 is not a coincidence — it's built into the math. Set the two formulas equal:

°C = (°C × 9/5) + 32  
°C − (°C × 9/5) = 32  
°C × (1 − 9/5) = 32  
°C × (−4/5) = 32  
°C = −40

So at exactly −40, both thermometers read the same number. This is useful trivia, but also a useful sanity check: if you ever see a conversion claiming −40 °C ≠ −40 °F, something went wrong.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is 100 °C in Fahrenheit?**
100 °C = 212 °F. This is the boiling point of water at sea level (1 atm of pressure). At higher altitudes, water boils at lower temperatures because atmospheric pressure is reduced.

**What is 37 °C in Fahrenheit?**
37 °C = 98.6 °F. This is the standard normal human body temperature, though healthy individuals vary between about 36.1 °C (97 °F) and 37.2 °C (99 °F) throughout the day.

**What is 0 °C in Fahrenheit?**
0 °C = 32 °F. This is the freezing point of water, and the offset built into the Fahrenheit formula (+32).

**Is there a temperature scale that uses absolute zero?**
Yes — the Kelvin scale (K) starts at absolute zero (−273.15 °C / −459.67 °F). It's used in physics and chemistry. To convert: K = °C + 273.15. There are no negative Kelvin values.

**Which scale is more precise?**
Neither scale is inherently more precise — both can be measured to as many decimal places as your thermometer allows. The Celsius degree is slightly larger than the Fahrenheit degree (1 °C = 1.8 °F), so Fahrenheit gives finer resolution for everyday temperatures without decimals.
