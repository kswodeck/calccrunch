About 20 states are holding a back-to-school sales tax holiday in 2026, with most falling between mid-July and late August — Alabama kicks things off July 17–19, Florida's holiday runs a full month from July 20 to August 20, and the biggest cluster of states (Arkansas, Massachusetts, Missouri, Ohio, South Carolina, Texas, and others) waive sales tax the first weekend of August. During the window, qualifying clothing, school supplies, and sometimes computers ring up completely tax-free.

Here's every confirmed date, what typically qualifies, and how to calculate exactly how much you'll save.

## What Is a Sales Tax Holiday?

A sales tax holiday (also called a "tax-free weekend") is a temporary period, usually set by state legislation, during which a state suspends its sales tax — and often local sales tax too — on specific categories of purchases. Back-to-school holidays almost always cover clothing and school supplies under a per-item price cap, and several states extend it to computers or even sports equipment.

The tax isn't refunded later — it's simply not charged at checkout on qualifying items during the holiday window, as long as you buy (and in most states, take delivery or place the order) within the dates.

### States With No Sales Tax Holiday

**Alaska, Delaware, Montana, New Hampshire, and Oregon** don't need a holiday — they don't charge state sales tax year-round. **California and New York** don't run back-to-school sales tax holidays at all, and a handful of states (Minnesota, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Vermont) permanently exempt most clothing from sales tax regardless of the time of year, which accomplishes a similar effect without a designated weekend.

## How Much Will You Actually Save?

The savings equal whatever your combined state and local sales tax rate would have been on the qualifying items. If your area's combined rate is 7% and you spend $300 on qualifying clothing and supplies, you save $21 — the exact tax you'd otherwise pay at checkout.

## Does It Actually Move the Needle?

For most families, a tax-free weekend saves real money but isn't the biggest lever — a store-wide clothing sale often beats the sales tax savings alone. The best approach is stacking both: shop during your state's holiday *and* during a sale, so you avoid the tax on top of an already-discounted price. Use the [discount calculator](/calculators/discount-calculator) to check the sale price first, then apply the tax-free savings on top of that.

**Example:** A $40 backpack is 20% off, bringing it to $32. Buying it at full price with a 7% sales tax would cost $42.80 ($40 × 1.07). Buying it on sale during the tax-free weekend costs exactly $32 — a total savings of $10.80 compared to paying full price with tax.

## Tips for Maximizing Tax-Free Weekend Savings

1. **Check your specific state's item list and price caps** — a $110 pair of shoes may not qualify if your state's cap is $100, and the excess amount is often fully taxed, not just the overage.
2. **Online orders usually qualify** — as long as the order is placed and paid for during the holiday window and ships to an address in a participating state, most major retailers apply the exemption automatically at checkout.
3. **Stack a sales tax holiday with clearance and back-to-school sales** for the biggest combined discount.
4. **Plan your list ahead of time** using a [budget calculator](/calculators/budget-calculator) so the tax savings go toward your total back-to-school budget instead of getting absorbed into impulse buys.
5. **Buy ahead for the next grade level** if your state's cap allows it — some families stock up on basics (notebooks, folders, backpacks) a year in advance since prices and tax rules reset annually.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I have to be in-state to get the tax exemption?**
No — the exemption applies based on where the sale takes place (or where an online order ships), not your state of residency. Visiting shoppers can benefit too, as long as they buy during the holiday window in a participating state.

**Does the exemption apply to online purchases?**
Generally yes. Most states apply the holiday to online orders as long as the order is placed and paid for during the holiday dates, regardless of when it ships or arrives.

**What happens if an item is over the price cap?**
In most states, if an item exceeds the price cap (for example, a $120 pair of shoes with a $100 cap), the entire item becomes taxable — not just the amount over the cap. A few states apply the exemption only up to the cap. Check your state's specific rule.

**Can I combine a tax-free weekend with store coupons and sales?**
Yes — tax-free weekends stack with any other discount. The sales tax is calculated on the discounted price, so combining a sale with the tax exemption maximizes total savings.

**Why do some states not have a back-to-school tax holiday?**
Five states charge no sales tax at all, so a holiday would have no effect. Others, including California and New York, have chosen not to adopt one, while a few states permanently exempt clothing from sales tax year-round instead of running a single weekend event.

## The Bottom Line

Back-to-school tax-free weekends run from mid-July through late August in 2026, with most states clustering around the first weekend of August. The savings are real but modest on their own — the bigger win comes from combining the tax exemption with an existing sale.
