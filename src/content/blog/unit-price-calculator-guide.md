Unit price is the cost of a product per single unit of measurement — per ounce, per pound, per liter, or per item. The formula is simple: **Unit Price = Total Price ÷ Quantity**. Once both products are expressed in the same unit, you can compare a 12 oz bag to a 32 oz bag on equal footing, and you'll often find that the "family size" package isn't actually the cheaper option.

Below is exactly how to calculate unit price by hand, plus a free [unit price calculator](/calculators/unit-price-calculator) that does the conversion for you and tells you which product wins.

## The Unit Price Formula

**Unit Price = Total Price ÷ Quantity**

That's it. The trick isn't the math — it's making sure both products are measured in the *same* unit before you compare them.

**Example:** A 12 oz bag of coffee costs \$8.99, and a 32 oz bag costs \$19.99.

- Bag A: \$8.99 ÷ 12 oz = **\$0.749/oz**
- Bag B: \$19.99 ÷ 32 oz = **\$0.625/oz**

The 32 oz bag is cheaper per ounce by about \$0.12 — roughly 17% less than the smaller bag, even though it costs more than twice as much upfront.

## Why You Can't Just Compare Sticker Prices

The whole reason unit pricing exists is that package sizes rarely line up. A \$4.50 bag of rice and a \$9.00 bag of rice aren't automatically "the same value per pound" — you have to know how much rice is actually in each bag.

| Product | Price | Quantity | Price per lb |
|---|---|---|---|
| Brand A rice | $4.50 | 1 lb | $4.50/lb |
| Brand B rice | $9.00 | 2.2 lb (1 kg) | ~$4.09/lb |

Here, Brand B is the better deal even though its sticker price is double — because you're getting more than double the rice. ## Comparing Products in Different Units

Grocery shelf tags are required by the FTC's Unit Pricing Rule to display a per-unit price, but stores aren't required to use the *same* unit for competing products — one brand might post price per ounce while the one right next to it posts price per pound. To compare them, convert both to a common unit first:

- **1 pound = 16 ounces**
- **1 kilogram = 35.274 ounces**
- **1 liter = 33.814 fluid ounces**
- **1 cup = 8 fluid ounces**

**Example:** Peanut butter A is \$4.99 for 16 oz. Peanut butter B is \$7.49 for 1.5 lb (24 oz).

- A: \$4.99 ÷ 16 oz = **\$0.312/oz**
- B: \$7.49 ÷ 24 oz = **\$0.312/oz**

They're a dead tie — knowing that saves you from agonizing over which jar to grab.

## Why Bigger Isn't Always Cheaper

It's tempting to assume the larger package always has the lower unit price, but that's not reliably true. Manufacturers know shoppers make this assumption, and "value size" or "family size" packaging is sometimes priced at a *higher* unit cost than the standard size — especially on sale weeks when the smaller size gets a bigger discount.

The only way to know for sure is to do the division. A few seconds with the [unit price calculator](/calculators/unit-price-calculator) beats guessing based on package size alone.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is unit price and how do you calculate it?**
Unit price is the cost per single unit of measurement (per ounce, pound, liter, or item). Formula: Unit Price = Total Price ÷ Quantity.

**Is the bigger package always the better deal?**
No. Bulk and family-size packages sometimes carry a *higher* unit price than the smaller size, especially during sales on the smaller package. Always check the actual unit price rather than assuming size equals savings.

**How do I compare products measured in different units, like ounces and pounds?**
Convert both to the same unit first. Since 1 pound = 16 ounces, a $4.50/lb product is $4.50 ÷ 16 = $0.281/oz. The [unit price calculator](/calculators/unit-price-calculator) does this conversion automatically for any two compatible weight, volume, or count units.

**Does unit price account for sales tax or coupons?**
No. Unit price only reflects the shelf price divided by quantity. Coupons, loyalty discounts, and sales tax all affect your final cost but aren't part of the unit price formula itself — factor those in separately once you know which product has the better base price.

**Why do two similar products show unit prices in different measurements on the shelf tag?**
Retailers are required to display a per-unit price, but the specific unit (per ounce vs. per pound, for example) isn't standardized between products, which makes mental comparison hard. Converting both to the same unit — as this calculator does — removes the guesswork.
